# Part IV: The Stance

*Where we stand.*

---

Vision and architecture mean nothing without integrity in execution. This part explains how we organize ourselves and why.

## Not Nonprofit

The obvious question: if the mission is about human flourishing and not extraction, why not structure as a nonprofit?

We considered it. Nonprofits have tax advantages. They signal mission purity. They attract grants and donors who want to support good work.

But nonprofits have constraints that conflict with our goals. Spending flexibility is limited. Boards can drift from founder intent. Donor influence becomes donor control. The same problem as venture capital, loss of sovereignty, dressed in different clothes.

This critique is not absolute. Khan Academy, Wikipedia, and Mozilla have maintained mission alignment at scale as nonprofits or foundations. The model can work. But it requires constant vigilance against board capture and donor dependence. We want a structure where alignment is built in, not continually defended.

So: not nonprofit.

## Not Extractive

The alternative trap is conventional venture-backed startup logic. Raise money, capture market, extract value, exit. The incentives are clear: growth metrics over mission, shareholder returns over user benefit, exit over endurance.

We reject this too.

The goal is not to build a company that gets acquired. The goal is to build infrastructure that outlasts us. Infrastructure does not have exits. It has stewards.

So: not extractive.

## Steward-Ownership

There is a third path.

Steward-ownership is a legal and governance structure designed for mission-driven companies that want to remain independent indefinitely. The model comes from the Purpose Foundation, which has worked with over 300 companies, and a growing ecosystem that includes Ecosia, Sharetribe, and Patagonia. Through Holdfast Collective, Patagonia has already directed $4.6 million to create the Vjosa River national park in Albania—profit serving planet, as promised.

This is not an untested model. In Denmark, steward-owned companies represent approximately 60% of the Copenhagen stock market's value. Novo Nordisk, one of the world's most valuable companies, is controlled by the Novo Nordisk Foundation. Carlsberg has been foundation-owned since 1888. Research shows Danish steward-owned companies have a 6x higher survival probability after 40 years compared to conventional peers—more resilient, more innovative, and equally profitable.

The core principles:

- **Profits stay in the mission.** The company can be profitable, pay fair wages, reinvest in growth. But profits cannot be extracted by owners beyond reasonable compensation.
- **No sale to extractive buyers.** The company cannot be sold to someone who would strip it for parts or pivot away from the mission.
- **Control stays with stewards.** Voting rights remain with people committed to the mission, not with whoever holds the most shares.

This structure has tradeoffs. Steward-owned companies cannot raise equity capital by design. This constrains growth speed and limits R&D investment. For infrastructure at civilizational scale, this may be a binding constraint. We accept the tradeoff because the alternative, ceding control to investors who may not share the mission, is worse. Slow alignment beats fast drift.

We cannot be acquired. This is usually framed as a constraint, but for infrastructure builders it is a strategic asset. Ecosystem partners can build on our systems knowing they will not be sold to a competitor. Contributors can trust that their verified credentials will exist in a decade. The inability to exit signals permanence, and permanence attracts the long-term partnerships that infrastructure requires.

The end goal, eventually: give the company to the planet. A cooperative structure where users and contributors share ownership of the infrastructure they helped build. Cooperatives face their own challenges: free-rider problems, decision paralysis, capital constraints. We do not have a complete governance design. What we have is a commitment to stewardship over extraction, with the intention to distribute ownership as we learn what works.

We know this is the difficult path. It is also the right path.

## Why We Don't Dictate

Part III acknowledged that compensation is unsolved. This is not an oversight. It is a refusal.

A founder dictating the compensation mechanism would replicate the extraction problem at a different layer. The whole critique of surveillance capitalism is that a small number of actors made decisions about value distribution without the consent of those whose value was being distributed. We will not repeat that pattern by decreeing how contributors should be paid.

These are collective decisions. They require deliberation, contestation, and legitimacy that no founder—however well-intentioned—can provide alone. You cannot have a legitimate answer to "how should contributors be paid?" without first answering "who decides?" The governance infrastructure must exist before the compensation mechanism can be decided.

We are early. There is work to do.

This is the purpose of DreamTree's profitability. Not personal enrichment. Not growth for its own sake. Revenue funds the creation of deliberative infrastructure: the forums, processes, and institutions through which these collective decisions can be made. We will fund the forums, moderate the debates, and implement what emerges—but we will not predetermine the outcome. We are building the preconditions for a debate we refuse to foreclose.

The contribution economy cannot be designed by one person and a swarm of AIs. It must be argued into existence by the people who will live in it.

## Open Source, Copyleft

The code is open. License: AGPL-3.0.

Why AGPL specifically? Because it is copyleft with teeth. If you use our code to build a service, you must share your modifications. This prevents the common exploitation pattern: take open-source work, build proprietary product on top, capture the value, give nothing back.

This choice has costs. AGPL discourages commercial adoption. Many enterprises refuse to integrate AGPL-licensed code because the copyleft requirement conflicts with their proprietary strategies. If our flywheel depends on enterprise demand, this creates friction.

Our answer: the core infrastructure should remain open, but we can provide alternative licensing for specific enterprise use cases where AGPL creates genuine obstacles. Dual licensing is established practice. The principle is that the commons remains common; the accommodation is that not every use case fits neatly into copyleft.

Open source is not charity. It is strategy. It builds trust. It invites contribution. It makes the code more robust through many eyes. And it ensures that even if this organization fails, the work survives. The infrastructure belongs to everyone.

## Movement, Not Empire

The data sovereignty movement exists. MyData Global has a declaration translated into 13 languages that influenced the Finnish Government Programme and the European Data Strategy. The Solid Project, led by Tim Berners-Lee, has built infrastructure for personal data stores. Verifiable Credentials have a W3C standard. AT Protocol has a growing ecosystem.

We are not the first to care about these problems. We do not need to reinvent every wheel.

Our stance: join the movement, don't dominate it. Adopt existing standards where they fit. Contribute to standards where they fall short. Build relationships with aligned organizations. Speak at their conferences. Publish in their channels. Become a champion for ideas bigger than ourselves.

What happens when existing standards conflict with our vision? We adapt where we can. We propose changes where we cannot. We do not fork lightly, but we also do not compromise core principles for compatibility. This is a judgment call, made repeatedly, in context.

The ego move is to build everything from scratch, claim credit, capture the space. The strategic move is to strengthen the ecosystem that strengthens us.

## Bootstrap

We are not raising venture capital.

VC money comes with expectations: rapid growth, market capture, exit. Those expectations shape every decision, often invisibly. The board seat that seemed harmless becomes the lever that redirects the mission. The growth metric that seemed neutral becomes the target that corrupts the product.

Bootstrap means slower growth. It means saying no to opportunities that require scale we cannot afford. It means profitability matters from the start, not as a post-IPO afterthought.

It also means freedom. Freedom to prioritize users over metrics. Freedom to build for decades, not quarters. Freedom to remain aligned with the mission because no one else is in the room demanding otherwise.

Mission-driven companies attract talent that conventional companies cannot. Patagonia reports 4% annual turnover—three times lower than the retail industry average—and receives 9,000 applicants for each open position. Research shows organizations with engaged, purpose-aligned employees outperform competitors by 147% in earnings per share. The decision to prioritize mission over exit is not merely principled. It is a talent strategy.

The revenue model: institutions pay. High schools, community colleges, bootcamps, career centers, workforce development boards. These organizations have budgets for career services. They need outcomes they can measure and report. DreamTree provides both: a tool that users value and data that institutions can use to demonstrate effectiveness.

The pricing is per-seat or per-institution, depending on scale. One contract serves hundreds of users. Retention is structural: institutions do not churn the way individual consumers do. Business-to-business sustains the mission. Free access is the mission.

This is not a complete business plan. It is a sketch. We will learn from customers what they value and price accordingly. What we commit to is the model: institutional revenue subsidizes free access.

---

This is who we are. Not nonprofit, not extractive. Steward-owned, open-source, movement-aligned, bootstrapped. Choosing the difficult path because the easy paths lead somewhere else.
