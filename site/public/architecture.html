part iii · the architecture

Philosophy without architecture is wishes.

Part II made the case for a contribution economy. This is how we build one — three layers, each enabling the next, assembled from standards that already exist and are already in production.

Layer one: the workbook

The entry point is a career coaching system: comprehensive, research-backed, free. Three parts, fifteen modules, sixty exercises. Not a quiz, not an AI wrapper — the distillation of seven years of work into a tool that rivals what executives pay thousands for.

Why give it away? Because the clarity that McKinsey partners buy, a 22-year-old in debt deserves too. Because the marginal cost of sharing knowledge is zero. And because the workbook is not the product. It is the door. Everyone who completes it generates structured self-knowledge: skills verified through reflection, values surfaced through exercises, stories built through guided prompts. That data belongs to them.

Layer two: the wallet — and this one is live

The infrastructure layer turns human contributions into portable, cryptographically verified assets. This is not a roadmap item. roots, the wallet on the dreamtree network, runs in production today at id.dreamtree.org, and the code is public.

built

W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0 with data-integrity proofs. Decentralized identifiers anyone can resolve. Consent checked on every read and logged for the owner. Verification recomputed cryptographically at the moment you look. Append-only correction. Full signed export. A learning platform (Telekora) already issues into it in production.

The standards underneath have quietly matured while the attention economy exhausted itself: decentralized identity (Bluesky's AT Protocol demonstrated portability with over 20 million users), verifiable credentials (a W3C standard since May 2025), selective disclosure through zero-knowledge proofs, content provenance through C2PA (now shipping in Leica and Nikon cameras, Adobe and OpenAI services, BBC images).

The contribution economy does not require new inventions. It requires assembly.

Layer three: the library

The foundation layer addresses the deepest problem: the amnesia of intelligence. AI models consume human knowledge but do not preserve its lineage. The Great Library restores the link through a provenance ledger (an immutable, tamper-evident record of creation, attestation, transformation, citation), a knowledge graph (“who created this?” becomes answerable), and a semantic space (similar ideas cluster; conceptual evolution becomes visible).

Four proofs anchor it: proof-of-origin, proof-of-rigor, proof-of-use, proof-of-replication. The principle is simple: knowledge should be free to use, but never free of its history.

The flywheel

People arrive for career clarity and generate structured contributions. With consent, contributions become credentials they own. Institutions issue verifiable records because compliance and funding increasingly demand provable outcomes. Employers reduce hiring risk with portable, verified skills. AI developers, facing litigation and regulation, pay for provenance-rich data — a market that already exists: Shutterstock made $104 million in 2023 licensing to OpenAI, Google, and Meta; Reddit earns $60 million a year from Google. And when contributions generate value, some portion flows back to the people who made them.

The flywheel turns because participation is rewarded, not because altruism is demanded.

What could go wrong

We should name the risks, because pretending they don't exist is how projects like this fail.

bootstrapping is hard

Network effects need critical mass. Our answer is the workbook: valuable enough to attract people regardless of the larger system, generating the seed of the network.

compensation is unsolved

We are building the infrastructure that makes compensation possible; the economic model that makes it actual is still emerging. We are not building from nothing: ASCAP and BMI distribute over $1 billion a year to songwriters from verified play data. If contribution can be tracked, compensation can follow.

adoption may not come

Incumbents have advantages. Users are fatigued. Institutions are conservative. These risks are reasons to proceed with humility and adapt when evidence contradicts expectation — not reasons to accept the extractive status quo. We decline.

The human stakes

A 22-year-old graduates with debt and a credential that does not distinguish them from a hundred thousand others. A mid-career professional is displaced, their experience locked in a former employer's database. A creator's work is scraped, absorbed into a model, and regenerated without attribution.

These are the people the contribution economy is for. Not because they are victims, but because they are contributors who have been systematically denied recognition. The technology is means, not end. The end is a world where contribution counts.
