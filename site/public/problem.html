part i · the problem

You cannot revolt against an algorithm you don't understand.

You cannot debate a neural network that governs credit access, employment visibility, or political discourse. If tyranny today is informational, then resistance must be epistemic — a battle for clarity, participation, and conscience. What follows is not a complaint. It is a diagnosis.

The single failure

Four crises dominate the digital economy. They appear unrelated. They are not.

The exhaustion of attention. For two decades, platforms have harvested behavioral data, packaged predictive insights, and sold them to advertisers. Shoshana Zuboff named the system precisely: surveillance capitalism — the unilateral claiming of human experience as free raw material for behavioral prediction.

$1.1T

Global advertising expenditure now exceeds $1.1 trillion annually, over 73% of it digital. The efficiency gains have plateaued. The trust is gone. And the costs are not only economic: the U.S. Surgeon General found adolescents spending more than three hours daily on social media face double the risk of depression and anxiety. The average teenager spends 3.5.

The anxiety of displacement. Unlike previous technological revolutions, AI encroaches on cognitive and creative domains once thought uniquely human. The promise was productivity. The reality is what Guy Standing calls the precariat: a class defined by instability, eroded trust, and mounting anxiety about the future.

40%

The IMF estimates 40% of global employment is exposed to AI-driven automation. 71% of Americans expect AI to permanently reduce jobs. Among AI researchers, 73% are optimistic about AI and work. Among the public: 23%. That gap is not ignorance. It is people noticing who the system was built for.

The extraction gap. This started before the internet. From 1948 to 1979, productivity and pay grew together. Then they came apart.

73.9% vs 17.1%

From 1979 to 2023, net productivity rose 73.9% while the hourly pay of typical workers grew 17.1% (Economic Policy Institute). The digital economy widened the gap: every search query trained a model, every tagged photo improved recognition, every scraped creative work now powers generative AI. The humans who generated that value received none of the returns.

The trust crisis. Employers face unverifiable credentials. Skilled people without formal credentials remain invisible. Institutions cannot prove outcomes. Resume inflation is rampant, and workplace surveillance expands because employers cannot otherwise measure contribution.

79%

79% of Americans do not trust companies to use AI responsibly. AI developers face litigation for training on unlicensed data. The EU AI Act now requires provenance disclosure. Anthropic settled for $1.5 billion — roughly $3,000 per book, half a million times over. Truth of origin now has a market price.

The fifth crisis: the dissolving of the real

There is a crisis the first four feed, and it is accelerating fastest. Generative AI has made the marginal cost of plausible content effectively zero. Text, images, voices, video, credentials, whole identities — all can now be produced at scale by systems with no stake in whether any of it is true.

When anything can be generated, nothing is self-evidently real. The question shifts from “what does it say?” to “who attests to it, and what did they stake?”

The world is already responding in fragments. The C2PA provenance standard now ships inside Leica and Nikon cameras, Adobe and OpenAI services, BBC newsroom images. Regulators demand disclosure of what trained what. Courts price the theft of origin. Each is a partial answer to the same question: in a world of synthetic everything, how do we know what a human actually did?

This is why provenance is not a compliance feature. As synthetic everything becomes free, verifiable provenance of real human accomplishment becomes the scarce asset. The record of what you actually did, attested by parties who staked something on saying so, may soon be the only kind of record worth anything at all.

A fair reader will object

has the attention economy not also created value?

Yes. Search engines organize human knowledge. Platforms enable connection. Free services have genuine utility. The critique is not that platforms create no value. It is that the distribution of value is systematically misaligned.

is AI displacement not overstated by historical precedent?

Perhaps. Mechanization displaced farmers; labor markets eventually adapted. But the speed of AI, and its reach into cognitive work, may outpace adaptation. We do not know. What we know is that the anxiety is real, and institutions have no good answer for it.

are credentials truly unverifiable, or merely imperfect?

Degrees provide some signal. The system functions, imperfectly. But “functions imperfectly” is not the same as “works well.” None of this is good enough.

These objections are serious. They do not refute the diagnosis. They qualify it.

One cause

The systematic failure to recognize, verify, and fairly reward human contributions in a digital economy increasingly shaped by AI.

The digital economy was built on extraction because extraction was easy. Attention could be harvested without consent. Data could be scraped without attribution. Value could be captured without compensation. The architecture permitted it, and so it happened.

The question is not whether this architecture is broken. It is. The question is what replaces it — and who will shape it, and toward what ends.
